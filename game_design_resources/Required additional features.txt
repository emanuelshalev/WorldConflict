Below is a structured design document defining the additional features for the expanded, global remake.
Scope and Core Vision This remake generalizes the original regional simulator into a global political, economic, and military simulation. The player may assume leadership of any country in the world, starting in a chosen historical year, and attempt to maximize a composite leadership score while navigating realistic international dynamics.
Key pillars:
Global scope: all countries, not just the Middle East.
Multi‑perspective play: any country is playable.
Asymmetric information: each country sees a different world.
Agentic simulation: each country is driven by an autonomous agent aligned with its history, structure, and culture.
Narrative framing: each playthrough begins with a story of how the player came to power.
Fast turn‑based flow with optional conversational decision support.
Rich end‑of‑term leadership report and scoring.
Playable Countries and Global Scope 2.1 Country Coverage All UN‑recognized sovereign states plus special entities as needed (e.g., EU, Taiwan).
Non‑playable but simulated entities:
Supranational organizations (UN, EU, NATO, regional blocs).
Major non‑state actors if desired (insurgent groups, multinational corporations) as simplified agents.
2.2 Country Profiles For each country, define a structured data model:
Identity:
Name, ISO codes, flag, location (polygons, centroids).
Political system:
System type (parliamentary democracy, presidential, authoritarian, monarchy, theocracy, hybrid, etc.).
Power centers (executive, legislature, judiciary, military, party apparatus, royal family, religious councils).
Election cycle and mechanics (term length, electoral system).
Historical context:
Key historic events (wars, revolutions, regime changes, alliances).
Baseline foreign policy orientations (alliances, rivalries, spheres of influence).
Cultural traits:
High‑level traits influencing behavior (risk tolerance, emphasis on prestige vs. stability, attitude to international norms).
Internal cleavages (ethnic, religious, ideological).
Economy:
GDP, sector breakdown (agriculture, industry, services).
Major resources and key exports.
Level of development, debt profile.
Military:
Force structure (branches, doctrines).
Nuclear status (none, latent, armed).
Alliance commitments (mutual defense treaties, basing agreements).
These profiles provide parameters for both the player’s options and the autonomous agents’ behavior.
Starting Year and Narrative Onboarding 3.1 Player‑Chosen Starting Year Player selects:
Starting year (e.g., any year from 1950 to near‑present, or scenario years).
Country to lead.
Game engine:
Initializes global state using historical data up to that year (alliances, wars, economic levels, regimes).
Generates existing leadership and institutions consistent with the chosen date.
3.2 Auto‑Fabricated Leadership Story Upon game start:
Narrative generator:
Creates a short backstory of how the player’s character came to power, consistent with:
Country’s political system (election, coup, appointment, revolution, succession).
Recent historical events (economic crisis, war loss, popular movement).
Example:
In a parliamentary democracy: “After a snap election triggered by a corruption scandal, your reformist coalition formed a narrow majority…”
In a military junta: “Following internal infighting, you emerged as the compromise general to lead the Supreme Council…”
Mechanical hooks:
Initial legitimacy level (high/medium/low).
Initial faction support (e.g., military backing strong, unions weak).
Public expectations (reformist mandate vs. stability mandate) affecting early score calculation.
Turn Structure and Decision Interface 4.1 Fast Options‑First Turn Flow Each turn (e.g., one month or quarter):
Situation Brief:
Quick summary of key developments (domestic, foreign, economic).
Highlighted alerts (crises, opportunities).
Action Options List:
Context‑aware menu of major decisions available this turn, grouped by:
Foreign Policy & Security.
Domestic Policy & Economy.
Social & Cultural Policy.
Intelligence & Covert Ops.
Designed to be concise: each option is a short, meaningful action (e.g., “Propose ceasefire in X war”, “Announce anticorruption campaign”, “Increase interest rates”, “Negotiate base rights with Y”).
Resolution:
Once an action is chosen, the turn continues to resolution (with underlying simulations and AI reactions).
4.2 Chat‑Driven Decision Support In addition to choosing from the options list, the player can open an in‑game “conversation” with characters before committing:
Available characters per turn:
Cabinet members (Foreign Minister, Defense Minister, Finance Minister, Interior Minister).
Intelligence chief, military chiefs, central bank governor.
Political allies/opposition leaders, influential business or civil society figures.
Foreign counterparts (via diplomatic channels) when relevant.
Conversation mode:
Player enters a chat view with the selected character.
They can:
Ask for analysis of the current situation (“If we sanction this neighbor, what happens?”).
Request alternative proposals.
Explore consequences of different actions.
The conversation persists until the player chooses a concrete action (from suggested or standard options), which then advances the turn.
Constraints:
No time passes until an action is locked in, preserving fast turn pacing.
Characters’ advice is biased by their incentives, ideology, and information (they do not see the full “truth” state, only their country’s view and their institutional lens).
Autonomous Country Agents 5.1 Agent Architecture Each country is controlled by an autonomous agent with:
State:
Internal metrics (stability, public opinion, factions).
Perceived world state (see Section 6).
Strategic goals and constraints.
Policy preferences:
Derived from:
Government type.
Cultural traits.
Historical experience.
Current leadership style (hawkish/dovish, reformist/conservative).
Decision logic:
A combination of:
Long‑term objectives (survival, prestige, economic growth).
Short‑term pressures (elections, protests, wars, sanctions).
Heuristics or utility functions (e.g., balancing risk vs. reward of escalation).
5.2 Behavioral Consistency The agent should:
Respect institutional constraints:
Democracies face electoral cycles, coalition politics, judicial review, free media.
Autocracies rely on elite support and fear coups or uprisings.
Theocracies or party‑states follow doctrinal or party‑line constraints.
React to fictional events plausibly:
Apply similar reasoning to both “realistic” and procedurally generated crises.
Example: A natural disaster may trigger requests for aid, temporary truce initiatives, or domestic budget shifts.
Maintain recognizable national “character”:
A country with historically cautious foreign policy tends to avoid large offensive wars unless under extreme pressure.
A country with an ideology of regional leadership pushes for alliances, regional institutions, and prestige projects.
Asymmetric Information and World Views 6.1 Ground Truth vs. Country Perceptions Game manager maintains:
The full, objective world state (true troop positions, covert operations, real economic stats, genuine public opinion values, etc.).
Each country maintains its own belief state:
Based on:
Public information (media, official announcements).
Intelligence gathering (spies, satellites, economic data).
Alliances and shared intelligence.
Belief state can be:
Outdated (lagging behind real events).
Incomplete (some variables unknown).
Incorrect (misinformation, propaganda, deception).
6.2 Information Channels News and media:
Global news events visible to most countries (major wars, coups, disasters).
Domestic media shaped by regime type (more propaganda in authoritarian states).
Intelligence:
Intelligence budget and capabilities influence:
Accuracy and timeliness of military, political, and economic data.
Ability to detect covert actions (e.g., coup attempts, nuclear programs).
Diplomacy:
Direct communication (summits, calls, diplomatic cables) can correct or distort perceptions.
Public leaks and whistleblowers:
Random or triggered events may reveal hidden information (corruption scandals, hidden treaties), updating some countries’ belief states.
6.3 Gameplay Impact Divergent beliefs:
Countries may act on false assumptions (e.g., overestimating enemy strength → deterrence; underestimating → miscalculated wars).
Misinformation operations become meaningful (e.g., faking military build‑ups).
Player experience:
As leader, the player only sees their country’s view:
Intelligence maps derived from their own data.
Uncertainty ranges (e.g., “Enemy GDP likely between X and Y”).
Actions to improve information:
Increase intelligence spending.
Seek data‑sharing deals with allies.
Support international monitoring missions.
Goals, Scoring, and Leadership Evaluation 7.1 Core Objective Primary goal: stay in power as long as possible while maximizing a multi‑dimensional leadership score.
7.2 Score Components Leadership achievements:
Economic performance (growth, unemployment, inflation control).
Security outcomes (avoiding catastrophic wars, defending territory).
Strategic gains (beneficial treaties, influence in international organizations, prestige projects).
Crisis management (handling pandemics, disasters, financial crises).
Internal approval:
Average domestic approval rating over time.
Fairness of approval (e.g., avoiding extreme repression; high approval under brutal coercion may be scored differently).
Fulfillment of electoral or ideological promises.
Global approval:
Standing in international organizations.
Global opinion indices (press freedom, human rights, corruption perception).
Reputation as a responsible or reckless actor (e.g., use of WMDs, frequency of wars, treaty violations).
7.3 Scoring Mechanics Continuous scoring:
Score accumulates each turn based on state changes, with modifiers for difficulty and country starting conditions.
Event multipliers:
Major achievements or disasters give large positive or negative score spikes.
Normalization:
Scores adjusted for country size and baseline challenges (e.g., leading a small, poor state vs. a superpower).
End‑of‑Term Report and Saveable History 8.1 Triggering End of Leadership End conditions:
Voluntary retirement or electoral loss.
Coup, assassination, revolution, impeachment.
Country collapse or annexation.
The simulation may continue, but the “run” for this leader ends.
8.2 Report Contents At the end of a run, generate a structured, exportable report:
Timeline of major events:
Key dates and descriptions (wars launched, treaties signed, crises managed, coups, elections).
Policy summary:
High‑level overview of domestic and foreign policy directions (e.g., “Market‑liberalization and non‑alignment”, “Militarized nationalism”).
Score breakdown:
Final total score.
Component scores (economic, security, internal approval, global approval).
Historical comparisons (how this leader ranks vs. typical leaders for this country, if multiple playthroughs exist).
Leadership style evaluation:
Qualitative classification:
Examples: “Pragmatic stabilizer”, “Reformist democrat”, “Risk‑taking expansionist”, “Isolationist nationalist”.
Justification referencing key decisions.
Charts and maps (in‑game, not necessarily in the exported file at first):
Approval over time.
GDP and key economic indicators.
Military expenditure and conflict involvement.
8.3 Export and Sharing Saveable format:
Structured data file (JSON/HTML/PDF, depending on platform) summarizing the leadership period.
Optional “narrative history”:
Short textual history written in the style of a post‑hoc political biography.
Map and Intelligence Visualization 9.1 2D Map Engine Global map:
2D, fast‑rendering, Google‑Maps‑style (pan, zoom).
Countries shown as filled polygons with borders; major cities, key infrastructure optionally visible.
9.2 Layer System Each layer can be toggled on/off; multiple layers can be combined for richer insights.
Military layer:
Known or estimated positions of friendly and foreign forces.
Alliance boundaries, bases, conflict zones.
Uncertainty visualization (e.g., fuzzy edges or confidence intervals for enemy deployments).
Economic layer:
GDP by country (color gradient).
Trade flows (arrows or lines with thickness representing volume).
Debt, reserves, key commodities (oil, gas, rare earths) as icons or heat maps.
Resources layer:
Natural resource locations and intensities (oil fields, mines, agricultural zones).
Strategic infrastructure (pipelines, ports, chokepoints).
Political & social layer:
Regime type shading.
Protest hotspots, insurgency regions.
Influence zones (soft power, spheres of influence).
Environment / climate (optional expansion):
Disaster risk zones.
Ongoing environmental crises (droughts, floods, fires).
9.3 Country‑Specific Intelligence View Player’s perspective:
The map shows only what their country believes:
Friendly regions with high certainty.
Foreign regions with partial or outdated information.
Fog‑of‑information:
Unknown regions shaded differently or annotated with “insufficient data”.
Intelligence investment reduces fog over time, especially for areas of interest.
Integration With Original Design Intent While the scope expands from a regional to a global simulator, the design keeps:
Turn‑based, information‑rich decision making.
Emphasis on political, diplomatic, and covert tools, not just raw warfare.
High stakes around escalation and global perception, including the possibility of catastrophic outcomes (e.g., nuclear war, systemic collapse).
The features above collectively define a modern, globally scoped, agent‑driven remake that preserves the spirit of the original while significantly deepening immersion, replayability, and systemic richness.
